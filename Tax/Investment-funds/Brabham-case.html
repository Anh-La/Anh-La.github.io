  2. GST Treatment:

    • The acquisition of the Brisbane Property should not be subject to GST as the transaction is considered a GST-free supply of a going concern.
    • For the ground floor units that are vacant at settlement, a 10% GST will apply. However, full input tax credits should be available for this GST on the assumption that these areas will be leased post-settlement, although this will increase the stamp duty cost.
    • It is crucial that ABC is registered for GST before settlement to ensure the GST-free going concern treatment.

  3. Deductibility of Construction Costs:

    • The construction cost of the building should be deductible as part of capital works deductions, typically at a rate of 2.5%. Further analysis may justify a higher rate of 4%, particularly if it can be demonstrated that the property use qualifies as a “hotel”.

Capital Works Deductions (Division 43 Deductions)

4% vs. 2.5% for Capital Works Deductions

  1. Standard and Higher Rates:

    • The standard rate for Division 43 deductions is 2.5%. A higher rate of 4% may apply if the Property is used as a hotel, motel, or guest house. However, if the Property is used as an apartment, the 4% rate does not apply.

  2. Determining Property Use:

A hotel is characterized by sole occupancy rooms where the primary offering is the accommodation area itself (bedroom). In contrast, an apartment typically features multiple facilities and services within the accommodation area, such as a kitchen, laundry, dining, and living areas, in addition to the bedroom.

  3. Hotel Operation:

    • To determine the applicable rate for capital works deductions, it is necessary to understand how the Hotel is being operated at the Property. Further consideration and analysis are required to confirm the use of the 4% rate.

  • Examples of Land Subject to AFAD Residential Land Surcharge:

    • Established homes and apartments, including self-contained studio apartments.

    • Land designated for residential development, such as small unit block developments, housing subdivisions, and major developments with a residential component.

Foreign Resident Capital Gains Withholding (FRCW)

Overview and Obligation

  • Purpose and Requirement:
    • The FRCW imposes an obligation on purchasers to withhold 12.5% of the purchase price and remit it to the Australian Taxation Office (ATO) when a vendor sells certain asset types, such as the property in question.
    • This withholding is not an additional tax but a mechanism to ensure tax collection from vendors, particularly those who are foreign residents.

Conditions and Compliance

  • Australian Resident Vendor:

    • If the vendor is an Australian resident, they can avoid the withholding requirement by providing the purchaser with a clearance certificate from the ATO before settlement.
    • The clearance certificate confirms that the vendor is an Australian resident, thereby exempting the transaction from the FRCW withholding requirement.

  • Obligations of Purchasers:

    • Purchasers, regardless of their residency status (Australian resident or foreign resident), are required to withhold the specified amount and pay it to the Commissioner of Taxation at settlement.

Recommendations and Precautions

  • Sale Agreement and Documentation:

    • Given the nature of the vendor group involved in the transaction, it is crucial to address the FRCW requirements in the Sale Agreement.
    • It is prudent to ensure that the Sale Agreement explicitly outlines the obligations regarding FRCW, and that appropriate measures are taken to comply with these obligations.

  • Residency Certificate:

    • It is recommended to obtain a residency certificate to confirm the vendor's status and to ensure that the purchaser’s obligations under the FRCW are met.

Ongoing Taxation of ABC as a Flow-Through Entity

Classification and Structure

  • Flow-Through Treatment:
    • ABC should be treated as a ‘flow-through’ entity for Australian income tax purposes.
    • Certain rules in the Australian tax legislation classify some widely held trusts that carry on trading activities as deemed corporate taxpayers. However, these rules are not expected to apply to ABC.

Public Trading Trust (PTT) Consideration

  • Division 6C of the ITAA 1936:

    • Under Division 6C, a trust may be taxed as a company if classified as a Public Trading Trust (PTT).
    • A trust is considered a PTT if it is a Public Unit Trust (PUT) and a trading trust that is either resident in the income year concerned or was a PTT in a previous income year.

  • Public Unit Trust (PUT) Criteria:

    • A trust is a PUT if its units are offered to the public or held by 50 or more persons.
    • Units are considered offered to the public if an offer or invitation is made to the public to subscribe or purchase units.

  • Concentration Test:

    • A unit trust is not a PUT if, at any time during the year, 20 or fewer persons hold 75% or more of the beneficial interests in the income and property of the trust.
    • Interposed trusts are generally required to be traced through when applying the concentration test.

  • Assessment of ABC:

    • Based on the proposed structure and background facts, ABC is not likely to satisfy the conditions to be considered a PUT and therefore, should not be classified as a PTT.
    • The analysis of whether ABC is a trading trust has not been considered.

Investment and Distribution Considerations

  • Investment Structure:

    • The investment is tightly held by High Net Worth Individuals (HNWI) and DPM.
    • The Commodity Murabaha is not a qualifying membership interest, indicating that ABC is unlikely to satisfy Managed Investment Trust (MIT) requirements and will not be eligible for the lower rate of 15%.

  • Tax Rate on Distributions to Hold Co:

    • Hold Co, as a beneficiary, should have a present entitlement to the distributable income of ABC each income year.
    • The trustee of ABC is liable to pay tax on behalf of the non-resident beneficiary (Hold Co) based on the rates for non-resident corporate beneficiaries (currently 30%). This tax is creditable to Hold Co in determining its Australian taxable income.
    • It is not projected for there to be taxable distributions of this type during the period.

  • Expectations for Excess Losses:

    • This expectation may need to be addressed in supporting the applicable interest rate (see unitholder loan discussion below).

Distribution Policy

Availability of Input Tax Credits and TOFA Regime for ABC

Input Tax Credits for GST

Taxation of Financial Arrangement (TOFA) Regime

  • Overview of TOFA Provisions:

    • The TOFA provisions determine the amount and timing of the taxation of gains and losses for financial arrangements. This does not include financial arrangements that are specifically excluded by the TOFA rules or those that do not exceed 12 months in term.
    • Under the TOFA rules, gains are assessable, and losses are deductible on a revenue account to the extent they are incurred in generating assessable income.

  • Precedence of TOFA Rules:

    • TOFA rules in Division 230 take precedence over other provisions in the income tax legislation and apply to the financial arrangements of an entity that exceeds specific economic thresholds outlined in section 230-5.

  • Economic Thresholds for TOFA Application:

    • Entities are excluded from the TOFA regime if they have:
      • Aggregate turnover of less than $100 million.
      • Financial assets of less than $100 million.
      • Total assets of less than $300 million.

  • TOFA Status for New Entities:

    • For newly created entities like ABC, the TOFA status for the first year of income is determined by applying the economic threshold at the end of the first year the entity comes into existence.

  • Election into the TOFA Regime:

    • Entities not mandatorily subject to the TOFA regime can elect to have the TOFA rules apply to them. This election is made in the first income tax return of ABC and is irrevocable.
    • Once subject to Division 230, all financial arrangements entered into by the entity from the relevant income year onwards will be governed by the TOFA rules.

  • ABC’s Position on TOFA:

    • When considered in isolation, ABC does not meet the thresholds for the mandatory application of the TOFA regime. However, potential associate rules may apply.
    • To provide certainty and more accurately reflect the underlying economic substance of financing arrangements, it is recommended that an election be made into the regime.

Australian Thin Capitalisation Regime

Tax Issue and Comments on the Australian Thin Capitalisation Regime

  1. Debt Structure and Deductibility:

    • ABC’s purchase of the Brisbane Property is likely to be 60% debt-financed through a conventional non-Shariah compliant debt structure from the Commonwealth Bank of Australia (CBA).
    • The Commonwealth Bank senior debt is considered a relevant debt interest under Australia's income tax laws.
    • Financing costs paid by ABC under the senior debt are deductible for tax purposes, subject to Australia’s thin capitalisation rules.

  2. Thin Capitalisation Provisions:

    • Not all Australian entities are subject to thin capitalisation provisions; the de minimis test applies if total debt deductions are A$2 million or less.
    • Australian entities are subject to thin capitalisation provisions if ‘controlled’ by a foreign entity.
    • ABC, owned and controlled by the non-resident entity Hold Co, is treated as foreign-controlled and is subject to thin capitalisation provisions.
    • The thin capitalisation rules limit the Australian debt deductions for foreign-controlled Australian entities (like ABC) when debt funding exceeds the entity’s maximum allowable debt level.

  3. Maximum Allowable Debt Levels:

    • The maximum allowable debt for trusts with foreign unit holders is the greater of:
      • The Safe Harbour Debt Amount
      • The Arm’s Length Debt Amount
      • The Worldwide Gearing Debt Amount

  4. Safe Harbour Debt Amount:

    • Broadly, the current safe harbour debt amount is equal to 60% of an Australian entity’s average adjusted assets less non-debt liabilities, translating to a 1.5:1 debt-to-equity ratio.
    • ABC’s safe harbour debt amount is calculated based on its total funding and senior debt from CBA.
    • ABC is likely to be geared beyond the safe harbour limit at inception, with a total funding of $27.14M and senior debt of $14.595M.
    • Without the de minimis exemption or the arm’s length debt test, ABC is likely to have no excess debt capacity at the acquisition of the Brisbane Property.

De Minimis Exemption and Associate Entities

  1. A$2 Million Threshold – De Minimis Exemption:

    • The thin capitalisation regime does not apply if the total debt deductions of an entity and its associates for an income year are A$2 million or less.
    • ABC’s total debt deductions, including interest on the CBA loan, interest on the unit holders' loan, and payments under the Commodity Murabaha, are expected to be less than A$2 million annually.
    • The de minimis exemption may apply; however, the total debt deductions for all of ABC’s associate entities for the income year must be included.

  2. Assessment of Associate Entities:

    • The relationship between Hold Co and Fin Co is crucial. If Fin Co is an associate entity of ABC, all debt deductions from Fin Co, Capital Co entities, and Hold Co must be included in ABC’s total debt deductions.
    • Fin Co is considered an associate of ABC if it has a “majority voting interest” or “sufficient influence” over Hold Co.
    • Hold Co will be sufficiently influenced by Fin Co or other entities if its directors are expected to act according to the directions, instructions, or wishes of Fin Co.

  3. Application of Associate Entity Rules:

    • Hold Co, as a 100% owner of equity units in ABC, is an associate of ABC.
    • The assessment of sufficient influence involves reviewing all relevant documents, including the WAAD and advisory agreements.

  4. Support for the De Minimis Position:

    • To ensure Fin Co and Hold Co are not regarded as associate entities, the Commodity Murabaha must operate as an arm’s-length financing arrangement.
    • Capital Co, through Fin Co, must maintain an arm’s-length borrower/lender relationship with Hold Co, as Shariah compliance prohibits involvement in conventional debt transactions.

  5. Reasonably Arguable Position Paper (RAPP):

    • Preparation of a RAPP is advised to support the de minimis position and mitigate penalties if challenged by the Commissioner.
    • A reasonably arguable position can protect against penalties if the taxpayer took ‘reasonable care’ in complying with tax law.

Impact of Movements on De Minimis

  1. Factors Affecting De Minimis Exemption:

    • The A$2 million debt deduction threshold may be affected by:
      • Movement in interest rates, given the CBA facility’s floating rate
      • Reset events under the CBA facility
      • Unitholder loan ratio

  2. Arm’s Length Debt Test – Application to ABC:

Unit Holder Loan: Tax Issues and Considerations

Debt Versus Equity Classification of Shareholder Loan

  • Deductibility of Interest Expenses: Interest expenses paid by ABC to Hold Co for the unitholder loan are generally deductible when calculating its Australian taxable income, provided that the loan qualifies as a ‘debt interest’ under Australian income tax laws.

  • Criteria for Debt Interest Classification:

    • The financing arrangement must create an effectively non-contingent obligation for the issuer (ABC) to make payments that equal or exceed the amount received.
    • For loans with a term of 10 years or less, no discounting of returns is necessary to establish the loan as a debt interest, allowing it to be classified as such under the rules.

  • Connected Entity Rule:

    • A provision within the Australian debt/equity rules (section 974-80 of the ITAA 1997) stipulates that a loan, which would typically be treated as a debt interest, is classified as an equity interest if the scheme is designed so that the return is used to fund a return to another entity and that return is equity-like.
    • A ‘connected entity’ is either an associate of the entity or a member of the same wholly-owned group.

  • Potential Arguments and Concerns:

    • There may be an argument that the unitholder loan is structured so that interest payments fund a return to Fin Co, and subsequently to HNWI and DPM investors. However, it is arguable that the scheme should not be treated this way since the interest payments are only one part of Hold Co's total income.
    • According to the Commissioner’s guidelines (TD 2015/2), the entirety of the funding arrangement and income sources need to be considered to apply the connected entity rule.
    • It is unlikely, based on current tax rulings, that the Commissioner would apply section 974-80 to deny interest deductions on the unitholder loan to Hold Co.

  • Uncertainty and Proposed Changes: The application of the connected entity rule is uncertain, and legislative changes might occur following the Board of Taxation’s review of the debt/equity provisions. Monitoring the progress of these amendments is crucial.

Interest Payments – Withholding Tax

  • Withholding Tax Rate: A 10% withholding tax applies to interest payments made by ABC under the unitholder loan.
  • Timing of Withholding: Withholding is required on interest paid or payable, including interest reinvested or otherwise dealt with on behalf of the non-resident.
  • Reporting and Payment Processes: ABC must have processes in place to manage the timing of reporting and remittance of withholding tax on interest.

Transfer Pricing Support

  • Australian Transfer Pricing Regime: The regime requires robust documentation to ensure that the interest rate and the amount of deductions for the unitholder loan are justified.
  • Arm's Length Principle: The interest rate should reflect what would be expected between independent parties under similar conditions.
  • Economic Circumstances: The transfer pricing analysis should consider the economic circumstances of the loan, including the accumulation of losses, to avoid potential re-characterization of the loan as equity.
  • Prudent Interest Rate: It may be advisable to adopt an interest rate that demonstrates the potential for net taxable income over the life of the project.

Gains from the sale of units in ABC or interests in upstream entities may be subject to Australian Capital Gains Tax (CGT) at a rate of 30% if the entity is deemed 'land rich.' For CGT purposes, an entity is 'land rich' if more than 50% of its assets' market value comprises Australian real property or leases of Australian real property. The capital gain is calculated as the excess of capital proceeds over the CGT cost base of the equity interests. The CGT cost base should be adjusted for any tax-deferred distributions made by ABC to Hold Co.

Comments:
